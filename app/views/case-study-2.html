The situation

When I took overall responsibility for the Educate Against Hate campaign at the Department for Education, it was falling short of its mission. Research showed that our primary audiences of teachers and parents felt ill-equipped to discuss extremism and radicalisation with young people, even after a successful campaign launch a year earlier.

The campaign’s content was part of the problem. It was heavy on technical, arcane policing jargon which often provoked fear instead of the clarity and reassurance most parents and teachers need to discuss emotive topics with young people. Teachers worried about saying the wrong thing and parents lacked knowledge of how to spot vulnerabilities to radicalisation in their children. In short, a well-intentioned project wasn’t reaching the people it needed to serve due to a failure in understanding their needs.

My task

My challenge was to pivot from a campaign that sought to scare its users into compliance, into one that would build their trust and confidence in fulfilling their safeguarding responsibilities. The goal would be to build a new brand identity and content strategy based upon a deep understanding of our users, our subject matter and how to the two intersected. By doing so we could increase the percentage of teachers, school leaders and parents who reported confidence in tackling these issues with students

My actions

Using research to understand contextual empathy

I began with behavioural insight research to understand why our audience was disengaged. Interviews and surveys revealed that the campaign’s jargon-heavy, security-centric tone actually intimidated teachers and parents. Using these findings, I created personas (like Anita, a cautious teacher, and Mark, a concerned parent) to personify our users’ fears and articulate their needs.

Developing a theory of change

Next, I developed a new theory of change to reframe the campaign’s approach. We shifted from a “policing extremism” mindset to a safeguarding mindset. Instead of emphasizing threats, we defined success as empowering teachers and parents to support at-risk youth early.

Developing a language game

To change the narrative, I worked with a user researcher to co-design a friendlier lexicon. We swapped out official jargon for simple, compassionate language. For example, “radicalisation indicators” became “signs a young person might be struggling.” We tested these changes with educators and refined wording until the tone felt accessible and non-judgmental. The new vocabulary made our content feel far more human and supportive.

Reflecting our findings in our designs

I then overhauled both the content and the site’s UX. We introduced new content types tailored to user needs: short conversation guides for quick, practical tips and longer explainers and lesson materials for deeper learning. I collaborated closely with the UI designer and developer to simplify the microsite’s architecture around these resources. We reorganised pages by audience and topic, so users could intuitively find relevant guidance. We iterated on wireframes and prototypes with feedback from teachers and parents at each step, ensuring the interface was clear and accessible.

Testing and iteration

Before launch, I led multiple rounds of usability testing with real users. Watching teachers and parents navigate the revamped site highlighted some final tweaks – for instance, we added a prominent “Worried about someone?” button based on their feedback. By launch, every piece of content had been refined through user input. What started as our concept truly became a co-created product with the community it served.

The results

The impact was immediate and lasting. Six months after relaunch, follow-up surveys showed teacher confidence in discussing extremism jumped from 58% to 83%, and parent confidence from 68% to 91%. Platform engagement also surged, page views and resource downloads more than doubled, and average time on page increased by about 40%. Qualitative feedback was glowing – educators described the site as “reassuring,” and parents found it “easy to use in real life.” A campaign that once bred uncertainty had become a trusted, empowering resource. By staying human-centred and collaborative throughout, I had turned a struggling initiative into one that truly supports its users and helps safeguard young people.
