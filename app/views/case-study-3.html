The situation

When I joined NHS North East London as Head of Digital Communications and Branding, I inherited a small but committed team of five content and communications professionals. Despite their dedication, they had little to no experience in content design or user-centred practices. Content requests arrived through a chaotic mix of emails, meetings, and hallway conversations. Much of what the team produced were inaccessible PDFs, published without clear guidance or standards. There was no content triage, no ticketing system, and little understanding across the organisation of what good digital content looked like—or why accessibility mattered.

My task

My task was twofold: first, to equip my team with the mindset and skills of content designers, and second, to build a broader culture of accessibility and user-centred thinking across the organisation. We needed to move from reactive content production to strategic, standards-driven content design—without losing momentum on our day-to-day publishing demands.

My actions

Upskilling my team in content design and UCD

I started by building internal confidence. I curated and wrote a comprehensive set of content design and accessibility guidance, drawing from GOV.UK and NHS standards. It covered everything from plain English principles to the case for user needs over organisational messaging. I also enrolled the entire team in FutureLearn’s Content Design course, giving them structured, shared learning to build from. We introduced weekly learning sessions, where team members shared insights and reviewed live content through a content design lens. Over time, these conversations shifted from “What do we need to say?” to “What does the user need to know, and how do we say it clearly?”

Providing structure to how we worked

We implemented a ticketing system to triage and track content requests, making workloads visible and helping us prioritise based on impact and user value. This small operational change made a big difference: it allowed us to plan content more strategically, say no when appropriate, and spot recurring user needs.

Creating a UCD culture

Recognising that we couldn’t do this alone, I took the message to the wider organisation. I developed and launched a style guide tailored to NHS NEL, embedding accessibility and content design best practices. Then, I presented at our all-staff meeting (over 3,000 people) making the case for content design not as a luxury, but as a core part of equitable, effective healthcare communication. I used real examples of inaccessible content and showed how a user-centred approach could improve outcomes for patients and staff alike.

First iteration and new aims

These changes helped build senior leadership trust. With their backing, I’m now scoping a complete redesign of our website using the NHS front-end framework. This will allow us to move away from legacy systems and design an experience that’s accessible, mobile-optimised, and structured around real user journeys.

The results

The transformation has been both cultural and measurable. Using the Silktide accessibility audit tool, we improved our score from 31% to 78% within six months. PDF reliance has decreased significantly, replaced by accessible web content. My team has moved from passive producers to proactive advocates—regularly supporting each other to apply best practice and pushing back when requests don’t meet our standards.

More broadly, the wider organisation has taken notice. Other teams now come to us asking for advice on how to write accessibly or design better content. Leadership understands the value of what we do—and is investing in it. The new website project is just the beginning of what I hope will be a long-term shift: one where user needs, not institutional habits, define how we communicate.

By investing in people, processes, and persuasion, we’ve begun to replace fragmentation with clarity, and compliance with care. And we’re only just getting started.
